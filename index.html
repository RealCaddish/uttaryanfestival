The Uttarayan Festival in Ahmedebad, India: Celebrating Where Earth Meets Sky through Kite-flying

A map by Nathaniel Deaton in the UKY Digital Mapping Program

The 14th of January is an exciting time to visit Ahmedebad if you happen to find yourself in India. The public holiday is celebrated throughout India to remind farmers that harvest season is approaching, and to venerate the Hindu deity, Surya, the solar deity popular in the Indian states of Gujarat, Rajasthan, Bihar, and many others. The Uttarayan Festival or Makarsankranthi is distinct from other traditional Indian holidays in that it is marked by the solar transition to Capricorn. In Hindi, Mankar is Capricorn and sankranthi means transition so this first Indian festival of the new year literally translates straight from the Indian astrological calendar.

Makarsankranthi is traditionally celebrated as a means for farmers to discard bad memories of the previous season's harvest and celebrate positivity and excitement for the fresh, upcoming season. The Uttarayan festival comes to life for two days wherein participants fly kites throughout all the major cities and villages throughout the Indian subcontinent. Kite-flying is therefore the mechanism by which veneration and celebration takes place, reaching high into the skies for Surya's agricultural blessing. Festival participants can expect to find the skies flooded with kites of various sizes, eat special sweets such as chikki - made with peanuts, sesame seeds, and dried fruit, and sweet khichda. The rooftops in the major cities are excellent vantage points for the festivities because here, participants often set up large sound systems and spend both days congregating and celebrating.

The map at the top of the page is centered on Ahmedebad in Gujarat Province. I decided to use Ahmedebad as the point of interest for Makarsankranthi because the city is one of the most popular places for the festival throughout India. Even more particular, the riverfront of the Sambarti is one of the best vantage points to experience all of the festivities, and if you're interested in finding kites and materials to make kites, in purple I've included some of the highest rated shops throughout the 'old city' to find what you're looking for. Zoom into the Sambarti River to find riverfront parks that are worth visiting during your trip!

Ahmedebad is a beautiful city and is worth exploring even outside of the festival celebrations. Gandhi's original ashram is located on the riverfront just north of Flower Park and now serves as an exciting museum for tourists seeking to learn more about his impact on India's independence in 1947.
